Split Pay vs. Flex: what property managers need to know

Both Split Pay and Flex let residents pay rent in two payments instead of one — but the experience for property managers and residents is very different. The most important difference isn't pricing. It's who actually gets approved.

Flex uses a credit-based approval model. Residents apply, Flex pulls their credit, and most are rejected — industry estimates put Flex's approval rate at 12–15%. That means if you offer Flex, roughly 85% of residents who try to use it will be turned away. For many property managers, the tool never gains traction because the resident experience at signup is a dead end for the majority.

Split Pay uses cashflow-based underwriting — no credit check, no hard pull. Approval takes 5–10 seconds. Roughly 50% of residents qualify, and those who don't fully qualify can start at a partial split (20–30%) and earn more access over time. The gap in reach is significant: 3–4× more of your residents can actually use Split Pay than can use Flex.

The setup difference: minutes vs. weeks

Getting Flex live at a property requires signing a PM contract and — for a seamless experience — integrating with your property management software (AppFolio, Yardi, Entrata, RentCafe, etc.). While Flex claims under 24 hours to get live in some cases, full PMS integration typically takes longer, and non-integrated properties require residents to manually enter rent amounts each month.

Split Pay requires nothing from the property manager. Residents get a virtual bank account and routing number, which they enter into your existing resident portal. Payments arrive as standard ACH — indistinguishable from any other bank transfer. No contracts, no integration, no accounting changes. You can share a flyer today and have residents signed up tonight.

Resident support: the hidden cost of choosing Flex

One of the most frequently cited complaints from property managers using Flex is that resident support questions end up on their desk. When residents can't reach Flex support directly, they call the leasing office. Split Pay offers four direct resident support channels — phone, live chat, email, and SMS — so residents always have somewhere to turn that isn't you.

Pricing: closer than it looks, but Split Pay wins at lower rents

Split Pay charges $9.99/month + 1.5% of rent. Flex charges $14.99/month + 1% of rent. On a $1,000 rent, both cost the resident $24.99/month. On a $1,500 rent, Flex is slightly cheaper ($29.99 vs $32.49). On a $750 rent, Split Pay is cheaper ($21.24 vs $22.49). The pricing is comparable — but Split Pay's lower base fee benefits residents in more affordable markets.

For property managers, both tools are free. Split Pay additionally offers a $100 Amazon gift card when the first resident in a building uses Split Pay, plus $3–5/resident/month through the Partner Program. Flex offers no PM revenue share.

Is Split Pay or Flex better for property managers?

For most property managers, Split Pay is the stronger choice: higher approval rates, no setup required, same-day launch, four resident support channels, and a PM revenue share program. Flex may be appropriate if you manage large luxury properties with high average rents and already have an established Flex integration in place. In most other scenarios — especially for managers who want to offer rent splitting without any operational lift — Split Pay wins on every practical dimension.
